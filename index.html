What is Safeguarding?

Safeguarding means protecting people, especially vulnerable individuals, from harm, abuse, and neglect. It ensures their well-being and upholds their human rights.

Key Principles:
  • • Prevention: Stopping harm before it happens
  • • Empowerment: Supporting people to make their own decisions
  • • Proportionality: Responding appropriately to the level of risk
  • • Partnership: Working together for the best outcomes
Everyone has a responsibility to safeguard others, not just professionals.

Signs to Watch For

Look out for these warning signs that someone may need help:

Physical Signs:
  • • Unexplained injuries or bruises
  • • Poor hygiene or unsuitable clothing
  • • Sudden weight loss or malnutrition
Behavioral Changes:
  • • Withdrawal or fearfulness
  • • Reluctance to go home or be with certain people
  • • Sudden changes in behavior or mood
Other Concerns:
  • • Sudden changes in finances or possessions
  • • Isolation from friends and family
Remember: Trust your instincts. If something doesn't feel right, it's worth reporting.

Action Steps

If you have concerns about someone's safety, follow these steps:

Immediate Response:
  • • Stay calm and listen carefully
  • • Reassure them they did the right thing speaking up
  • • Do not promise confidentiality
  • • Ask open questions, don't interrogate
Documentation & Reporting:
  • • Record what was said as soon as possible
  • • Use their exact words where possible
  • • Report your concerns to appropriate authorities
Emergency: If someone is in immediate danger, call 999 immediately.
Non-emergency: Contact local safeguarding services or use our contact form below.

Safeguarding in Practice

Essential knowledge for creating safe environments and responding effectively to concerns

How to Recognise Concerns

Physical Indicators:
  • Unexplained injuries or marks
  • Frequent accidents or injuries
  • Poor personal hygiene
  • Inappropriate clothing for weather
  • Signs of malnutrition
Behavioral Changes:
  • Sudden changes in behavior
  • Withdrawal from activities
  • Fear of specific people/places
  • Regression in development
  • Self-harm behaviors
Remember: One sign alone may not indicate abuse, but multiple indicators or your gut feeling that something isn't right should prompt action.

How to Respond

Immediate Response Steps:
1
Stay Calm

Don't show shock or disbelief. Your reaction can affect whether the person continues to share.

2
Listen Actively

Give your full attention. Don't interrupt or ask leading questions.

3
Reassure

Tell them they did the right thing by speaking up and that it's not their fault.

4
Document

Write down what was said using their exact words as soon as possible.

5
Report

Contact appropriate authorities or use our reporting system below.

DON'T: Promise to keep secrets, investigate yourself, or confront the alleged perpetrator.

Safeguarding Best Practices

Creating Safe Environments
  • • Maintain open communication channels
  • • Implement clear policies and procedures
  • • Ensure proper supervision ratios
  • • Regular safeguarding training for all staff
  • • Background checks for all personnel
  • • Clear reporting mechanisms
Online Safety
  • • Monitor online activities appropriately
  • • Educate about cyberbullying
  • • Set clear digital boundaries
  • • Use privacy settings on social media
  • • Report inappropriate online behavior
  • • Keep personal information private
Supporting Vulnerable People
  • • Respect their dignity and choices
  • • Involve them in decisions about their care
  • • Recognize signs of self-neglect
  • • Understand capacity and consent
  • • Provide emotional support
  • • Connect them with appropriate services

  • You will not get in trouble for reporting genuine concerns

Key Principles

Key principles of safeguarding include prevention, empowerment, and partnership.

What is Safeguarding?

Safeguarding is a comprehensive approach to protecting the health, wellbeing, dignity, and human rights of individuals who may be vulnerable to harm, abuse, or neglect. It encompasses a wide range of activities designed to promote safety, prevent abuse, and respond effectively when concerns arise.

Core Definition

Safeguarding means taking action to enable all individuals to live safely, free from abuse and neglect. It involves protecting people's health, wellbeing, and human rights, and enabling them to live free from harm, abuse, and neglect.

Key Principles
  • • Empowerment: People being supported and encouraged to make their own decisions and give informed consent
  • • Prevention: It's better to take action before harm occurs
  • • Proportionality: The least intrusive response appropriate to the risk presented
  • • Protection: Support and representation for those in greatest need
  • • Partnership: Local solutions through services working together
  • • Accountability: Transparency in safeguarding practice
Who Needs Safeguarding?
  • • Children and young people: Under 18 years old
  • • Adults at risk: 18+ who have care and support needs and are experiencing, or at risk of, abuse or neglect
  • • Vulnerable adults: Those with physical or mental disabilities, illness, or impairment
  • • Older people: Particularly those with dementia or physical frailty
  • • Anyone in temporary vulnerability: Due to circumstances, isolation, or dependency
Types of Abuse
  • • Physical abuse • Sexual abuse • Emotional/psychological abuse
  • • Neglect • Financial abuse • Domestic violence
  • • Modern slavery • Discriminatory abuse • Organizational abuse
Remember: Safeguarding is everyone's responsibility - not just professionals. We all have a duty to protect those who may be vulnerable.

Signs to Watch For

Learn to recognize these warning signs that someone may need help:

Physical Warning Signs
  • • Unexplained injuries, bruises, cuts, or burns
  • • Frequent accidents or injuries with vague explanations
  • • Poor personal hygiene or unkempt appearance
  • • Inappropriate clothing for weather or situation
  • • Signs of malnutrition or dehydration
  • • Untreated medical or dental problems
  • • Flinching when approached or touched
  • • Evidence of poor supervision or abandonment
Behavioral Warning Signs
  • • Sudden changes in behavior, mood, or personality
  • • Withdrawal from activities, friends, or family
  • • Fear of specific people, places, or situations
  • • Regression to earlier behaviors (bed-wetting, thumb-sucking)
  • • Self-harm behaviors or suicide attempts
  • • Difficulty sleeping or frequent nightmares
  • • Loss of appetite or eating disorders
  • • Aggressive or violent behavior
Emotional Warning Signs
  • • Low self-esteem or self-worth
  • • Depression, anxiety, or emotional instability
  • • Difficulty forming relationships or trusting others
  • • Excessive compliance or people-pleasing behavior
  • • Inappropriate emotional responses to situations
  • • Lack of emotional expression or flat affect
Social Warning Signs
  • • Isolation from friends, family, or support networks
  • • Reluctance to participate in activities they once enjoyed
  • • Being prevented from accessing services or opportunities
  • • Someone else speaking for them or controlling their interactions
  • • Missing work, school, or appointments frequently
  • • Loss of independence or decision-making ability
Financial Warning Signs
  • • Sudden changes in financial situation or spending patterns
  • • Unexplained loss of money or valuable possessions
  • • Someone else controlling their finances or benefits
  • • Inability to access their own money or accounts
  • • Being pressured to change wills or legal documents
  • • Lack of basic necessities despite adequate income
Remember: Trust your instincts. If something doesn't feel right, it's worth reporting.

Action Steps

If you have concerns about someone's safety, follow these steps:

Immediate Response:
  • • Stay calm and listen carefully
  • • Reassure them they did the right thing speaking up
  • • Do not promise confidentiality
  • • Ask open questions, don't interrogate
Documentation & Reporting:
  • • Record what was said as soon as possible
  • • Use their exact words where possible
  • • Report your concerns to appropriate authorities
Emergency: If someone is in immediate danger, call 999 immediately.
Non-emergency: Contact local safeguarding services or use our contact form below.

Safeguarding in Practice

Essential knowledge for creating safe environments and responding effectively to concerns

How to Recognise Concerns

Physical Indicators:
  • Unexplained injuries or marks
  • Frequent accidents or injuries
  • Poor personal hygiene
  • Inappropriate clothing for weather
  • Signs of malnutrition
Behavioral Changes:
  • Sudden changes in behavior
  • Withdrawal from activities
  • Fear of specific people/places
  • Regression in development
  • Self-harm behaviors
Remember: One sign alone may not indicate abuse, but multiple indicators or your gut feeling that something isn't right should prompt action.

How to Respond

Immediate Response Steps:
1
Stay Calm

Don't show shock or disbelief. Your reaction can affect whether the person continues to share.

2
Listen Actively

Give your full attention. Don't interrupt or ask leading questions.

3
Reassure

Tell them they did the right thing by speaking up and that it's not their fault.

4
Document

Write down what was said using their exact words as soon as possible.

5
Report

Contact appropriate authorities or use our reporting system below.

DON'T: Promise to keep secrets, investigate yourself, or confront the alleged perpetrator.

Safeguarding Best Practices

Creating Safe Environments
  • • Maintain open communication channels
  • • Implement clear policies and procedures
  • • Ensure proper supervision ratios
  • • Regular safeguarding training for all staff
  • • Background checks for all personnel
  • • Clear reporting mechanisms
Online Safety
  • • Monitor online activities appropriately
  • • Educate about cyberbullying
  • • Set clear digital boundaries
  • • Use privacy settings on social media
  • • Report inappropriate online behavior
  • • Keep personal information private
Supporting Vulnerable People
  • • Respect their dignity and choices
  • • Involve them in decisions about their care
  • • Recognize signs of self-neglect
  • • Understand capacity and consent
  • • Provide emotional support
  • • Connect them with appropriate services
